The Private Club Model & Its Mandates

Understanding Our 501(c)(7) Status

The Bona Fide Private Club

A 501(c)(7) is a legally recognized social or recreational nonprofit. Its primary purpose is to serve members, not the public, granting tax exemptions but also specific responsibilities.

Key Requirements

  • Nonprofit tax status: Exempt from federal income tax on member-generated income.
  • Primary member support: The majority of income must come from members.
  • Limited public access: Facilities are reserved for members and their guests.
  • No private inurement: No earnings can benefit a private individual.

"Private" vs. "Public"

Exempt from public accommodation laws, giving private clubs the legal right to be selective in membership.

Key factors for "private" status:

  • Member control of operations.
  • Selective membership process.
  • Limited public facility use.

Why Our Admissions Process is Deliberate

To Serve and Sustain the Community

The process ensures new members align with the club's values, protecting our cherished community.

To Fulfill Fiduciary Duty

We must ensure new members contribute positively, protecting the investment of current members.

To Ensure Tax Compliance

A selective process proves we are a private entity, a core requirement of our 501(c)(7) status.

Advertising

Pathways to Membership: A Quick Reference

No matter the pathway, all applicants must: Complete an application & background check, sign waivers, provide marriage certificate (if applicable), undergo a 30-day posting, and be reviewed by both the Membership Committee and Board of Trustees before an invitation is extended.

1. Waitlist

For new applicants. Requires Proposer + Seconder letters (or personal/professional letters if self-proposed) and a $2,500 deposit per adult.

2. Spouse/Partner

For partners of current members with proof of relationship. Bypasses waitlist and sponsor letter requirements.

3. Legacy

For children (26-35) of members. Requires proposer (family) and seconder (non-family), with potential for discounted rates.

4. Diversity Admissions

For diverse candidates active in the community. Requires nomination and DAC interview, with an option to defer initiation fees.

5. Athletic

For athletes completing a 3-5 year team agreement. Up to 50% of dues paid can be credited toward the initiation fee.

6. Scholar Athlete

For MAC Foundation award winners. Includes a 2-year temporary membership, with a scholarship applicable to a future initiation fee.

Initiation Fees vs. Dues: A Tale of Two Funds

Initiation Fees = Capital Investment

A one-time fee to fund the club's future. These funds are reserved for long-term capital projects like major renovations and expansions, representing a member's "buy-in" to the club's physical assets.

Dues = Operational Funding

A recurring monthly fee to power the present. Dues fund the entire day-to-day operation of the club, including staffing, programming, utilities, and routine maintenance—the lifeblood of the member experience.

The Significance of Being an "Elected Member"

This term is not ceremonial; it is a critical distinction defined in our Bylaws for an individual who has completed the entire formal admissions process. This status confers:

  • Governance & Ownership: The right to vote, serve on committees, and be elected to the Board. Elected members are the effective "owners" of the club.
  • Stewardship & Commitment: A long-term commitment to the club's financial health, culture, and future.
  • Responsibility: The duty to uphold Club Rules and act as a steward for the institution.

Our Philosophy: Curation Over Recruitment

The MAC does not offer financial incentives for sponsoring new members. This is a deliberate choice, rooted in our core principles of stewardship and fairness.

  • Integrity of Sponsorship

    We believe sponsorship is a responsibility—a personal testament to a candidate's character—not a transaction.

  • Equity in Admission

    Our process ensures every candidate proceeds on their own merit, maintaining a fair and consistent standard for all.

Member Retention: The Resignation Survey

A data-driven initiative to understand attrition, support retention efforts, and ultimately strengthen the MAC experience for all.

Methodology

A dynamic, confidential survey sent quarterly to a targeted list of 1,023 recently resigned members. All data is anonymized and handled with strict privacy protocols.

Feedback Loop

The process includes a seamless workflow for follow-up. If a respondent requests contact, an automated notification with their feedback is sent directly to the Membership team for a tailored response.

Appendix: Guide to the Articles of Incorporation

Article 1: Name and Duration
The Gist: This section gives the club its official legal name and states that it is a "mutual benefit corporation" intended to exist forever.
What It Means: Name: "Multnomah Athletic Club" ("MAC"). Duration: "Perpetual" duration means the club doesn't have a legal expiration date. Type: A "mutual benefit corporation" is a specific type of nonprofit. Its primary purpose is to serve its members, not the general public.
Application & Implications: This article establishes the club's fundamental legal identity. The "mutual benefit" status is key, as it dictates that the club's finances and operations must be for the good of its members.
Article 2: Purposes, Powers and Other Matters
The Gist: This is the club's "mission statement" in legal terms. It lists the six main reasons the MAC exists.
What It Means: The club's stated purposes are to: 1. Be an athletic club. 2. Promote physical fitness. 3. Manage all aspects of membership. 4. Collect money (fees, dues, fines). 5. Create and enforce its own rules (Bylaws). 6. Have "reciprocal relations" with other similar clubs.
Application & Implications: Every action the club or Board takes must be traceable back to one of these purposes. For example, building a new weight room is justified by 2.1.1 and 2.1.2. The club's code of conduct is justified by 2.1.5.
Article 3: Membership
The Gist: This article confirms that the MAC is a membership organization. It then delegates all the specific details about membership to the Bylaws.
Application & Implications: If someone has a question about membership (e.g., "How do I apply?", "What are the types of membership?"), the answer is not in this document. This article directs you to look in the Bylaws for all terms and conditions.
Article 4: Board of Trustees
The Gist: This article establishes the club's leadership structure. The club is managed by a 12-member Board of Trustees. All the details about their election and service are in the Bylaws.
What It Means: Size: The Board has twelve members. Responsibility: The Board's job is to "manage the affairs of the MAC". Process: All "how-to" details (eligibility, term length, elections) are delegated to the Bylaws.
Article 5: Limitation of Liability
The Gist: This is a legal protection for volunteer leaders. It states that Trustees and uncompensated officers cannot be held personally liable for monetary damages for decisions they make while doing their jobs.
Application & Implications: This protection helps the club recruit qualified volunteers to serve on the Board, as they are shielded from significant personal financial risk.
Article 6: Dissolution
The Gist: This is the "end-of-life" plan. If the MAC ever dissolves, all its debts must be paid first. Any remaining assets are then distributed to the members.
Application & Implications: This provision distinguishes the club from a public charity. If a 501(c)(3) charity dissolves, its assets must go to another charity. For the MAC, the assets go back to the members.

Appendix: Guide to the Club's Bylaws

Article The Gist
1: Organization & ManagementEstablishes the club's name and states that the Board of Trustees has the ultimate authority for management.
2: Members and MembershipsDefines membership classes, the formal admission process (proposers, posting, committee review, Board vote), and rules for resignation/suspension.
3: Meetings of MembersOutlines the rules for the Annual Meeting (when it's held, what's reported) and Special Meetings (who can call one). It also defines voting rights and quorum.

5: OfficersDefines the four officers (President, VP, Treasurer, Secretary), their duties, and that they are elected annually by the Board from the Trustees.
6: General ManagerStates that the Board hires, evaluates, and sets compensation for the General Manager, who is the chief operating officer responsible for all staff.
7: IndemnificationProvides legal protection (indemnity) to Trustees, officers, and the GM, ensuring they are not personally liable for good-faith decisions.
8: CommitteesEstablishes the standing committees (e.g., Athletic, House, Membership) as the advisory bodies that recommend policies to the Board.
9: Financial MattersBasic rules for how club funds are deposited and invested, as approved by the Board.
10: Guest PrivilegesGives members the right to invite guests, subject to Board regulation. The member is responsible for their guest's conduct and charges.
11: Club Colors and EmblemDefines the official colors (scarlet and white) and emblem (winged "M").
12: InterpretationLegal clarification that "including" means "without limitation" and "writing" includes electronic.
13: Amendment of BylawsThe two ways to change the Bylaws: 1) Proposal by a Trustee, posted for 60 days, passed by 3/4 of the Board. 2) Petition by 5% of members, passed by 2/3 of members voting at the Annual Meeting.
